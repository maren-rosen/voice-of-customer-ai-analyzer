## 4. Recommended Actions for the Marketing Team

### Action 1 — Overhaul Size Guidance with Customer-Generated Fit Data
Sizing complaints appear in **at least 18 of 50 reviews**, making it the single largest source of dissatisfaction. The marketing and product teams should:
- Add a **dynamic fit calculator** to each PDP (product detail page) that surfaces real customer measurements (height, weight, bust) submitted in reviews.
- Include explicit **"runs small / true to size / runs large"** badges, updated in real time as reviews accumulate.
- Highlight petite and plus-size-specific fit notes where relevant, as several reviewers flagged unmet needs in those segments.

---

### Action 2 — Lead Creative with Unique Design Details
Customers repeatedly praised distinctive features — embroidery, back details, lace, ruffles, and patterns — as key purchase drivers. Marketing should:
- Shoot **close-up detail photography** for all editorial and paid social assets to showcase craftsmanship that may be invisible in standard flat-lay or full-body shots (as Review #47 notes the design detail "barely shows" online).
- Use UGC (user-generated content) from enthusiastic reviewers as social proof in email campaigns and ads, particularly quotes that mention compliments received while wearing the item.

---

### Action 3 — Address Quality Perception Before It Becomes a Return Driver
Fabric and construction complaints (pilling, shrinkage, see-through material, broken zippers) risk both returns and long-term brand trust. The marketing team should:
- Proactively communicate **care instructions, fabric weight, and opacity** in product descriptions to set accurate expectations and reduce disappointment-driven returns.
- Work with the QA/merchandising team to flag recurring defect patterns (zippers, seam shrinkage) and, where quality is genuinely strong, use **specific material callouts** ("98% cotton," "pure wool," "chlorine-safe") as marketing proof points — customers who noticed these details reacted positively.
